Acute SARS-CoV-2 Infection in Pregnancy Is Associated with Placental ACE2 Shedding

How COVID-19 disrupts the ACE2 axis at the maternal-fetal interface

SARS-CoV-2 Placenta ACE2 Maternal-Fetal Interface COVID-19 in Pregnancy RAAS

Overview

Pregnancy represents a unique immunological state in which the maternal system must tolerate a semi-allogeneic fetus while maintaining defense against pathogens. SARS-CoV-2 infection during pregnancy was associated with a range of adverse outcomes — yet the molecular mechanisms at the placental interface remained poorly characterized.

This study demonstrates that acute SARS-CoV-2 infection during pregnancy is associated with shedding of placental ACE2, the primary entry receptor for SARS-CoV-2. This finding connects two important threads: the vulnerability of ACE2-expressing tissues to SARS-CoV-2 infection, and the broader role of ACE2 as a regulator of the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) at the maternal-fetal interface.

Connection to Broader Research

This work complements the group's findings in pulmonary vascular cells (Klouda et al., Angiogenesis 2022) showing that interferon signaling upregulates ACE2 in vascular tissue — together pointing to a systemic ACE2 dysregulation across multiple organ systems in COVID-19.

Background & Motivation

ACE2 is a carboxypeptidase that cleaves angiotensin II to angiotensin-(1-7), negatively regulating the vasoconstricting, pro-inflammatory arm of the RAAS. It is highly expressed in the placenta — particularly in syncytiotrophoblasts — where it plays roles in placental vascular tone, nutrient transport, and immune regulation.

When SARS-CoV-2 binds and enters cells via ACE2, it triggers receptor internalization and subsequent proteolytic shedding of the ACE2 ectodomain into the circulation. In the placenta, loss of surface ACE2 activity could shift the local RAAS balance toward a pro-inflammatory, vasoconstrictive state — potentially contributing to placental dysfunction and adverse pregnancy outcomes observed in COVID-19.

Experimental Approach

Clinical Cohort

Placental tissue and maternal serum/plasma were collected from a prospectively enrolled cohort of pregnant women with confirmed acute SARS-CoV-2 infection and gestational-age-matched uninfected controls at delivery.

ACE2 Shedding Quantification

Soluble ACE2 (sACE2) levels were measured in maternal plasma by ELISA. Placental ACE2 expression was assessed by immunohistochemistry and Western blot, and localized to specific cell types by immunofluorescence co-staining.

Placental Viral Detection

SARS-CoV-2 RNA and nucleocapsid protein were assessed in placental tissue by RT-PCR and immunofluorescence to determine whether the placenta itself was directly infected, or whether the ACE2 changes were a systemic response.

RAAS Axis Assessment

Angiotensin II and angiotensin-(1-7) levels were measured to determine whether ACE2 loss was functionally reflected in a shift in the local RAAS balance within the placental compartment.

Key Findings

Acute SARS-CoV-2 infection in pregnancy is associated with significantly elevated soluble ACE2 in maternal plasma and reduced placental surface ACE2 expression, consistent with virus-driven receptor shedding at the maternal-fetal interface.

  • Plasma sACE2 is significantly elevated in SARS-CoV-2-positive pregnancies compared to matched controls at delivery.
  • Placental ACE2 protein is reduced at the syncytiotrophoblast surface in COVID-19-affected placentas, with the loss concentrated in areas of histological injury.
  • SARS-CoV-2 RNA and nucleocapsid protein are detectable in a subset of placentas, confirming direct viral infection of placental tissue in some cases.
  • The shift in the angiotensin II / angiotensin-(1-7) ratio in affected placentas is consistent with functional loss of ACE2 carboxypeptidase activity.
  • ACE2 shedding correlates with markers of placental inflammation, suggesting it may contribute to — or reflect — local inflammatory injury.

Significance

These findings have several clinical and mechanistic implications:

  • Placental dysfunction: Loss of ACE2-mediated angiotensin II degradation could promote local vasoconstriction and inflammation, contributing to placental insufficiency and the elevated rates of preeclampsia-like syndrome observed in COVID-19 pregnancies.
  • Fetal risk: Disruption of placental RAAS regulation may affect fetal nutrient delivery and vascular development, with potential downstream consequences for fetal growth.
  • Biomarker potential: Elevated plasma sACE2 may serve as a circulating biomarker of placental ACE2 shedding in pregnant COVID-19 patients.
  • Mechanistic link: Combined with the group's findings in pulmonary vascular cells, these data suggest that ACE2 dysregulation is a conserved feature of SARS-CoV-2 pathology across multiple vascular/epithelial compartments.
