Precision Cut Lung Slices as an Efficient Ex Vivo Tool for Pulmonary Vessel Studies

Establishing and validating a reproducible platform for pulmonary vascular structure and contractility

PCLS Ex Vivo Vascular Contractility Pulmonary Vasculature Methodology Live Imaging Mouse Lung

Overview

Studying pulmonary vascular biology presents a persistent methodological challenge: isolated cell culture strips away the architectural and mechanical context that defines vascular function, while whole-animal preparations are too complex for controlled pharmacological interrogation. Precision-cut lung slices (PCLS) occupy a productive middle ground — a thin, viable ex vivo preparation that preserves vessel architecture, cell–cell contacts, and dynamic responsiveness to vasoactive stimuli.

This JoVE protocol paper establishes and validates PCLS as an efficient tool specifically for pulmonary vessel structure and contractility studies, providing a detailed, reproducible workflow from tissue preparation through live-imaging analysis that can be adopted by the vascular biology community.

Why PCLS? Advantages Over Alternatives

🏗️
Preserved Architecture

Vessels remain in their native extracellular matrix and retain perivascular cells (pericytes, smooth muscle cells) in proper spatial relationship.

⚡
Functional Responsiveness

Vessels in PCLS contract and dilate in response to vasoconstrictors (endothelin-1, KCl) and vasodilators (acetylcholine, NO donors), enabling pharmacological profiling.

🔬
Live Imaging Compatible

Thin slices (150–200 µm) are compatible with confocal live imaging, enabling real-time visualization of vascular tone changes and cell behavior.

🧪
Paired Conditions

Multiple slices from a single animal allow within-animal comparison of different drug conditions, reducing biological variability and animal use.

🐭
Genetic Model Compatible

PCLS from transgenic or knockout mice can be directly compared to controls, bridging in vivo genetics with ex vivo functional readouts.

📏
Size-Selectivity

Small pulmonary arteries (50–200 µm diameter) — the vessels most relevant to PAH — are well-preserved and accessible for targeted analysis.

Protocol Overview

The workflow as established in this paper proceeds in five main stages:

1
Lung Inflation & Agarose Instillation

Low-melting-point agarose is instilled into the lungs via tracheal cannulation to provide structural support during slicing. Critical: agarose temperature and concentration must be optimized to preserve cell viability.

2
Vibratome Slicing

Solidified lungs are sliced at 150–200 µm thickness using a vibratome. Optimal vibration frequency and advance speed parameters are reported for consistent, damage-free slices.

3
Agarose Removal & Recovery Incubation

Slices are incubated at 37°C in DMEM to liquefy and wash out the agarose while cells recover functional activity. Viability is confirmed by live/dead staining.

4
Contractility Assay Under Live Imaging

Slices are mounted in a perfusion chamber on an inverted confocal microscope. Vasoactive agents are applied and vessel lumen area is tracked over time to quantify contractile responses.

5
Post-Assay Fixation & Structural Analysis

Following functional assays, slices can be fixed for immunofluorescence (αSMA, CD31, NG2) to assess vessel wall composition and structural remodeling markers.

Validation & Key Results

PCLS prepared with this protocol maintain viability for 48–72 hours, show robust and reproducible vasoconstriction/dilation responses, and preserve the cellular composition of the pulmonary vessel wall.

  • Endothelin-1 (ET-1) induces dose-dependent vasoconstriction in small pulmonary arteries (EC50 within reported range for intact vessels).
  • ACh-induced vasodilation is endothelium-dependent, confirming functional endothelial integrity in the PCLS preparation.
  • Immunofluorescent co-staining of CD31 (endothelium), αSMA (smooth muscle), and NG2 (pericytes) confirms retention of all three vascular cell layers.
  • Inter-preparation reproducibility (coefficient of variation for ET-1 response <15%) validates the protocol for quantitative comparisons across experimental groups.
  • PCLS from hypoxia-exposed mice show significantly attenuated vasodilatory responses compared to normoxic controls, demonstrating disease-model utility.

Applications in Our Research

PCLS prepared with this protocol have been used throughout our subsequent research program as a central experimental platform:

  • Characterizing vasomotor dysfunction in hypoxia-induced pulmonary hypertension models (→ pericyte HIF2α project)
  • Assessing ACE2 expression in intact pulmonary vessel walls following IFN stimulation (→ SARS-CoV-2 vascular infection project)
  • Pharmacological profiling of candidate compounds targeting vascular remodeling
  • Live-imaging of pericyte–endothelial dynamics under hypoxic conditions
