IFN-α/β Paradoxically Facilitates SARS-CoV-2 Pulmonary Vascular Infection by Upregulating ACE2

How the antiviral immune response creates a pro-viral niche in the lung vasculature

SARS-CoV-2 Interferon Signaling ACE2 Pulmonary Vasculature Vascular Tropism COVID-19 Angiogenesis

Overview

The severity of COVID-19 correlates strongly with vascular pathology — coagulopathy, endothelialitis, and pulmonary vascular dysfunction. Yet how SARS-CoV-2 gains access to pulmonary vascular cells in the first place remained poorly understood. This work uncovers a paradoxical mechanism: type I interferons (IFN-α and IFN-β), the body's canonical antiviral defense, transcriptionally upregulate ACE2 — the receptor SARS-CoV-2 uses to enter cells — on pulmonary vascular endothelial and smooth muscle cells.

The Paradox

Interferon signaling is classically protective: it restricts viral replication and primes an antiviral state. But in the pulmonary vasculature, IFN-driven ACE2 upregulation increases the density of SARS-CoV-2 entry receptors — making vascular cells more, not less, susceptible to infection.

Background & Motivation

SARS-CoV-2 enters cells via ACE2, a transmembrane carboxypeptidase highly expressed in lung alveolar type II cells. However, clinical reports from COVID-19 patients documented widespread vascular pathology — thrombi in small pulmonary arteries, endothelial injury, and virally infected endothelial cells — in tissue where baseline ACE2 expression is low.

The question we asked: what induces ACE2 in pulmonary vascular cells during early SARS-CoV-2 infection? Type I interferons are among the first cytokines released in response to viral sensing, and the ACE2 locus contains interferon-stimulated response elements (ISREs). We hypothesized that IFN signaling, triggered by early viral sensing or bystander immune activation, upregulates ACE2 and thereby sensitizes the vasculature.

Experimental Approach

IFN Treatment of Vascular Cells

Human pulmonary artery endothelial cells (HPAECs) and smooth muscle cells (HPASMCs) were treated with recombinant IFN-α or IFN-β at physiologically relevant concentrations. ACE2 mRNA and protein levels were measured by qRT-PCR, Western blot, and immunofluorescence.

ACE2-Dependent SARS-CoV-2 Infection

IFN-pretreated and control vascular cells were exposed to SARS-CoV-2 pseudovirus and authentic virus in BSL-3 conditions. Viral entry, replication, and cell viability were quantified to determine whether IFN-driven ACE2 upregulation translated to enhanced infectivity.

Promoter Analysis

Bioinformatic analysis of the ACE2 promoter confirmed ISRE and IFN-γ activation site (GAS) elements, and chromatin immunoprecipitation validated STAT1/STAT2 occupancy at these sites following IFN stimulation.

In Vivo Validation

Mice treated with poly(I:C) to induce endogenous IFN responses were examined for pulmonary ACE2 expression by IHC, confirming the IFN→ACE2 axis in vivo in intact lung vasculature.

Key Findings

IFN-α and IFN-β dose-dependently upregulate ACE2 expression in pulmonary vascular cells, and this upregulation significantly enhances SARS-CoV-2 entry and replication in these cells.

  • Both IFN-α and IFN-β robustly increase ACE2 mRNA (5–15×) and protein (3–8×) in HPAECs and HPASMCs.
  • ACE2 upregulation is JAK1/TYK2-STAT1/STAT2-dependent; it is abolished by ruxolitinib (JAK inhibitor).
  • IFN-pretreated vascular cells show significantly higher SARS-CoV-2 pseudovirus entry compared to untreated controls.
  • Authentic SARS-CoV-2 replication is amplified in IFN-exposed HPAECs, with increased viral RNA and nucleocapsid protein detectable at 48 h.
  • In vivo, poly(I:C) challenge increases ACE2 expression specifically in pulmonary vascular endothelium, confirmed by co-staining with the endothelial marker CD31.
  • The IFN→ACE2 mechanism is also operative in placental cells, consistent with the companion observation of placental ACE2 shedding in COVID-19 pregnancies.

Significance

These findings reframe how SARS-CoV-2 achieves its vascular tropism. Rather than requiring high baseline ACE2, the virus exploits the host's own antiviral machinery: the early interferon burst that precedes adaptive immunity inadvertently opens the vascular door. This has several implications:

  • Therapeutic: JAK inhibitors, which block IFN→STAT signaling (and are already in clinical use for severe COVID-19), may partially work by limiting ACE2 upregulation in vascular cells.
  • Pathogenic: The timing of IFN responses relative to vascular spread could explain why endovascular disease predominates in patients with robust early innate immune activation.
  • Broader: Other IFN-inducing pathogens that also use ACE2 (or similar IFN-upregulated receptors) may exploit this same route of vascular entry.
